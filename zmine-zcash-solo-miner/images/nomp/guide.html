Overview

What Is Solo Mining?

When you mine ZEC, your hardware runs billions of mathematical calculations per second trying to find a specific number — a hash — that satisfies the network's current difficulty target. The entire Zcash network is doing this simultaneously. Whoever finds a valid hash first wins the block and collects the block reward. In solo mining, that entire reward goes to you and nobody else. No pool fee, no middleman, no cut — but also no guarantee of when you'll find one. It's pure probability.

Everything on this dashboard is designed to answer three questions: Is my node healthy? Are my miners working? What are my chances?

The difficulty target requires the hash to start with a certain number of zeros. Your hardware cycles through trillions of candidates. This process — Equihash 200,9 — is Zcash's proof-of-work algorithm. The hash that starts with enough zeros is your winning ticket.
Header Bar

The Top Bar at a Glance

The thin bar at the top of every page is your at-a-glance health summary. It stays visible as you scroll so you always know the state of your operation without having to hunt for information.

Status Indicator

The most important indicator on the page. It tells you the state of your Zebra full node. Mining is only active when the status is Live.

Live Syncing Offline
Live
Fully synced, 8+ peers, ready to submit blocks. This is what you want.
Syncing
Node is still downloading the blockchain, or has fewer than 8 peers. Mining is paused — you cannot win a block while syncing.
Offline
Node has lost connectivity entirely or the software has crashed. Nothing is working.

Peer Count & Your Node on the Network

The number below the Dashboard title shows how many other Zcash nodes your Zebra instance is currently connected to. Peers are how your node learns about new blocks and broadcasts its own. A healthy solo miner typically holds 10–20 peers. Below 8 is weak connectivity.

Why your peers matter to the worldwide Zcash network

Every node that stays online and reachable strengthens Zcash for everyone — not just for you. The Zcash network has no central server. It is a mesh of independent nodes, each holding a full copy of the blockchain and relaying transactions and blocks to its neighbours.

When your node accepts inbound connections from the outside world — rather than only dialling out — it becomes an entry point for other nodes that are just joining the network, syncing for the first time, or reconnecting after going offline.

MORE RESILIENT
Harder to attack or partition — more independent paths for blocks to travel
MORE DECENTRALISED
No single region, ISP, or datacenter dominates the peer graph
MORE TRUSTWORTHY
More independent verifiers makes fraudulent transactions harder to push
FASTER FOR NEWCOMERS
New nodes download the chain from whoever responds first — more reachable nodes means faster first syncs worldwide

Block propagation speed and the orphan race

There is also a direct, personal mining incentive to having fast, well-connected peers — not just a community one.

Occasionally two miners solve a valid block at almost exactly the same time. Both are technically correct, but the network can only keep one. It converges on whichever block reaches the majority of nodes first. The other block is discarded — called an orphan — and that miner earns nothing, even though they did real work.

Block propagation race — your block vs a competing block found simultaneously

The moment your node finds a block, it announces it to every peer simultaneously. Each peer relays it to their own peers, fanning outward in concentric rings. More peers means more simultaneous first hops. Lower latency means each hop completes faster. Both multiply together to determine how quickly your block reaches the tipping point.

!
For most home solo miners, orphan races are rare. But when you finally find a block — possibly after weeks of waiting — you want every possible advantage ensuring your block is the one the network accepts. Peer count and peer quality are the levers you actually control.

Impact on your home internet

Running a Zcash full node with inbound peers open has very modest bandwidth requirements. Typical usage is roughly 10–50 MB per day for block propagation and peer traffic — comparable to a few minutes of video streaming. The initial blockchain sync uses several GB total over the multi-day sync period, but once synced, ongoing traffic is minimal.

There is no meaningful performance impact on home internet use. Video calls, gaming, and streaming are unaffected. Upload bandwidth is used when your node serves blockchain data to peers requesting it — on most broadband connections this is inconsequential. If you are on a metered connection, worth monitoring for the first week after enabling inbound connections.

Enabling inbound peer connections — port forwarding

By default, Zebra dials out to other nodes but does not accept inbound connections because your home router blocks unsolicited traffic. To allow inbound connections, forward TCP port 8233 from your router to your Umbrel's local IP address.

Zcash P2P Port

TCP 8233
This is the only port needed for P2P node connectivity. Do not confuse it with port 13333 (the stratum port for your mining hardware).
1
Find your Umbrel's local IP. It appears in your Umbrel settings, or look in your router's connected-devices list. It will look like 192.168.x.x. Assign it a static/reserved DHCP lease so it doesn't change after a reboot.
2
Log in to your router's admin interface. Usually 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1 in a browser. Credentials are often printed on the router itself.
3
Find the port forwarding section. May be labelled "Virtual Server", "NAT", "Port Mapping", or "Applications & Gaming" depending on your router brand.
4
Create a new rule: External port 8233 → Internal IP (your Umbrel) → Internal port 8233 → Protocol TCP. Save and apply.
5
Verify it worked. Search online for "open port checker" and test port 8233 against your home's public IP. If it reports open, inbound connections are live. Your peer count will rise within a few minutes.
✦
Once port 8233 is forwarded, your node becomes a first-class citizen of the Zcash peer-to-peer network — not just a passive participant. Your block propagation speed improves, and you're actively contributing to network health worldwide.

Node & Sync

Blockchain Sync Card

This card appears only while your Zebra node is still downloading the blockchain. Once fully synced it disappears and the rest of the dashboard takes over.

Progress %
How much of the blockchain history your node has downloaded and verified, shown as a percentage inside the circular arc ring on the right side of the card. The ring fills clockwise as the chain downloads and breathes gently while sync is active. 100% means fully synced.
Block X / ~Y
Your current block height versus the estimated tip of the chain. The estimated total is approximate because new blocks keep arriving while you sync.
Chain Size
Disk space the blockchain currently occupies. As of 2026, a full Zcash sync requires roughly 60–80 GB. Make sure you have headroom on your storage volume.
Peers Connected
How many nodes Zebra is talking to during sync. More peers means faster block downloads — your node can fetch different segments of the chain from different sources simultaneously. A sub-line below the peer count shows the countries and cities of your connected peers, resolved via geo-lookup — a quick way to confirm that your node has geographically diverse connections rather than clustering in one region.
Latest Synced
A live feed of the most recently verified block heights, auto-scrolling as your node processes new blocks. Fills the full lower half of the card so you can watch sync progress in real time.
Wipe & Resync
Deletes all downloaded blockchain data and starts from zero. Use only if your data is corrupted or you ran out of disk space mid-sync. Irreversible — the entire multi-day sync restarts from block 1.
!
Mining only works after full sync. Zebra needs the complete blockchain to construct valid block templates. During sync, your stratum server accepts connections but will not issue real mining work.
Node & Sync

Stratum Card

Your mining pool's connection hub. It handles the communication layer between your Zebra node and your mining hardware.

Stratum Address
The connection string your ASICs or GPU rigs need. Format: stratum+tcp://YOUR-IP:13333. The dashboard auto-detects your Umbrel IP so you can copy the correct string directly. Status below the address reads either "synced — accepting connections" (green) or "waiting for sync" (amber).
Active Workers
Individual worker connections currently active. A "worker" is a single mining device. The sub-line shows the number of unique miner addresses — relevant if multiple people are mining to the same stratum with different reward addresses.
Connections (TCP)
Raw TCP connections to the stratum port. Under normal operation, TCP connections and active workers should match closely. A large gap (many connections, few workers) indicates miners connecting and disconnecting rapidly — usually a misconfiguration on the miner side.
Chain Storage
Path on disk where Zebra stores blockchain data and how large it currently is. Useful for storage planning and confirming Zebra is writing to the expected location.
Network Peers
Expandable section listing each peer your Zebra node is currently connected to, with the country and city resolved from their IP address. This is the same information summarised in the sync card during the download phase, but here it persists after sync is complete. A geographically diverse peer list — nodes spread across multiple countries and continents — means faster block propagation and better resilience against regional outages. The header always reads network peers regardless of which network (mainnet or testnet) you are running.

URL
Use the exact address shown in the Stratum card on the dashboard — it auto-detects your Umbrel's local IP. The stratum+tcp:// prefix is required by most modern miners. Older interfaces may ask for host and port separately: host is your Umbrel's IP, port is 13333.
Username
Your full Zcash address (starts with t1 or t3), a dot, then a worker name you choose. The address determines where block rewards are paid — use the same address shown in the Mining Address field on the dashboard. The worker name is just a label for this machine; use letters, numbers, and hyphens, keep it short. Examples: t1fgGy...ig6.asic-rig-1, t1fgGy...ig6.gpu-tower.
Password
Enter x. This pool does not authenticate workers by password — any non-empty value is accepted. x is the universal stratum convention. Do not leave it blank — some miners will refuse to connect without a password set.
!
Address mismatch warning: if you enter a different Zcash address than the one configured in the pool, the Workers table will show an amber ⚠ addr badge next to that worker. Block rewards from that worker will go to the address the miner submitted, not to your configured wallet.

Network Difficulty
The current target the entire Zcash network is mining against. Higher = harder for every miner on the planet. Your Fleet side shows your best share ever submitted — proof that your hardware is doing real work even when it hasn't found a block yet.
Projected Retarget
Zcash uses Digishield — difficulty adjusts on every single block. The projected number estimates what the next block's difficulty will be. A difficulty spike can push your estimated time to block significantly higher overnight.
Hash Rate
Network Sol/s (global total) vs your fleet's combined Sol/s. The line below the comparison ("Pool is X% of network") is your exact probability of winning any given block.
Share Cadence
How frequently your fleet submits shares, expressed as an average interval. This is your heartbeat monitor — if a miner stops submitting shares, it has stopped working. Cadence drops before hashrate drops in most monitoring systems, so it's often the first warning sign that something is wrong. A secondary line shows your fastest worker by name — for example ⚡ gpu-rig-1 (45s) — and its individual share interval. Only the short worker name is shown, not the full address, so it stays readable even with long wallet addresses. Useful for comparing performance between machines at a glance.
Mining Stats

Est. Time to Block

This card answers the question every solo miner actually wants answered: how long do I have to wait?

Statistical mean at current hash rate
21 h 34 m
network difficulty ÷ fleet Sol/s = expected seconds between blocks
!
This is a statistical average, not a guarantee. If the estimate is 21 hours, you should expect roughly one block per day on average — but some days you find two, some weeks you find none. The universe doesn't owe you one block per interval; it owes you that rate only over a very long statistical run.
Blocks Found
Cumulative count since the pool started — confirmed and pending. Mining pools typically require 100 confirmations before a block is fully confirmed, because occasionally a freshly-found block can be orphaned — replaced by a competing block found at nearly the same time.
Mining Address
The Zcash transparent address (t1... or t3...) where all block rewards are sent. Zebra includes it in every coinbase transaction. You can update it from the dashboard without editing config files.
Block Reward
ZEC awarded per block at the current height, alongside how many blocks remain until the next halving and an approximate date. Zcash halvings reduce the reward by 50% every 840,000 blocks (~4 years).
Halving Countdown
Progress bar filling toward the next halving. When the halving happens, the reward drops instantly at that exact block height. Halvings also tend to affect difficulty — miners exit when rewards shrink, which can temporarily lower network difficulty.
Mining Stats

Block Odds & Mining Progress

Chance Today / Month
The raw probability that your fleet finds at least one block in the next 24 hours or 30 days. A 4.4% chance today means: run this exact operation for 100 independent days and expect to find a block on roughly 4 of them. No optimism — pure math from your Sol/s and current difficulty.
Fleet Share Stats
Submitted — valid shares accepted by the pool across all workers, all time. Rejected — shares sent but refused (stale shares, difficulty mismatches, or bad hashes). Rej % — below 1% is excellent, 1–5% is acceptable, above 5% suggests a configuration or hardware problem.
Fleet's Best Share
The highest-difficulty hash any worker has ever submitted. Expressed as a % of the current network block difficulty. A best share of "48.2% of block difficulty" means your fleet's best moment ever got halfway there. The bar fills toward 100% — which means a block was found.
i
If your monthly block probability is under 1%, a mining pool is almost certainly the better financial choice. Solo mining rewards patience and scale — it becomes more viable as you add more hardware.
Market Data

ZEC / USD Price Card

The live ZEC price in US dollars with the percentage change over the last 24 hours, updated every minute. The sparkline chart shows ZEC's price history over the last 90 days, with 90-day high and low shown alongside. The sparkline doesn't tell you what to do — it's context. If you just found a block, it tells you whether this is a good moment in price history or a bad one.

Market Data

Network Difficulty

The same network difficulty shown in the VS panel, but charted over 90 days. One of the most strategically useful charts on the dashboard for solo miners.

RISING DIFFICULTY
More hash rate has joined the network. Your share of the pie is shrinking unless you add hardware to match.
FALLING DIFFICULTY
Miners have left. Your relative share grew and your expected time to block got shorter without you doing anything.

A large sustained rise in difficulty while your hardware stays constant is the signal that your solo operation is becoming less viable unless you upgrade.

Worker / Address
The name your miner was configured with — format is typically address.workername. Click to filter the table to just that worker. Two sub-lines can appear below: the hostname or IP of the machine as seen by the stratum server, and the RTT (round-trip latency) shown in colour — green under 20ms, amber 20–80ms, red over 80ms. High RTT increases stale shares.
Status + Uptime
Online (green dot) means the worker submitted a share in the last two minutes. Idle (grey dot) means nothing recent. A worker idle for over 10 minutes is almost certainly offline. The sub-line shows how long this worker has been continuously connected in the current session — resets on reconnect, useful for spotting unstable connections.
Hash Rate ↑↓
Estimated Sol/s, calculated from recent share frequency and difficulty. A live estimate that converges to the true rated hashrate over time. The trend arrow (↑↓) appears once enough samples accumulate — useful for catching gradual thermal throttling or a card intermittently dropping cores.
Fleet %
This worker's share of your total fleet hashrate. Instantly shows which machines are doing most of the work. Colour scales with contribution — bright for dominant rigs, dimmed for minor contributors.
Rej % ↑↓
Rejected shares as a percentage of total submitted for this worker. Below 1% is excellent, 1–5% is acceptable, above 5% suggests a problem. The trend arrow appears when the rate is changing — an upward trend on a previously clean worker is worth investigating before it gets worse.
Last Share
Time since this worker last submitted a share. "43s ago" is healthy. "12 min ago" on a worker that should be active is a warning sign.
All-Time Best
The highest-difficulty hash this worker has ever submitted — how close this individual machine has ever come to finding a block. A sub-line labelled session: shows the best hash since it last connected, resetting on reconnect.
% of Block
The all-time best share as a percentage of current network block difficulty. "31%" = this rig's best hash was 31% of the way to actually solving a block. Colour-coded dim-to-bright as it approaches 100%. When it hits 100%, a block was found.
℃ / Sol/W
Temperature and efficiency placeholders for a future direct miner API integration. Currently show "—" for all workers. When a miner API is available, these will show chip temperature and hash rate per watt — the two most important hardware health metrics for long-running rigs.

Your Fleet

Blocks Found Table — Blocks Tab

A complete history of every block your operation has ever found.

Height
The block number in the Zcash chain. On mainnet this links to a block explorer where you can verify the block and see the coinbase transaction. Higher numbers mean more recent blocks.
Status
Confirmed — fully accepted by the network, reward paid. Pending — found recently, waiting for enough confirmations (typically 100). Pending status usually resolves to Confirmed within a few hours. Occasionally a pending block gets orphaned — the network chose a competing block found at nearly the same time.
Miner / Worker
Which of your miners found this block. Address truncated — hover for the full string.
Block Hash
The 64-character hash that solved the block. Click to copy. On mainnet, this is the link target for the block explorer URL.
Coinbase TX
The transaction hash for the coinbase transaction — the special first transaction in the block that creates new ZEC and sends it to your mining address. This is proof of your reward. Look it up in any block explorer or your wallet to confirm receipt.
Time Found
How long ago this block was found, shown as a relative time ("3 days ago"). Hover for the precise UTC timestamp.
